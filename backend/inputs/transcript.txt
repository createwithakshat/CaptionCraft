 So, when you do opt for coaching, what outcomes can you expect? First is greater awareness, and second is stronger accountability. You create awareness by helping the employees see what's really happening, what good looks like, and what's getting in the way.  Think back to the example of Alex. You begin by giving Alex clear, fact-based feedback to reveal the pattern of behavior. For example, you say, I've noticed you haven't yet incorporated the new AI tools into your workflow. This could affect your efficiency over time. Or, in our last meeting, when teammates shared an example of how the tools saved time, join the discussion.  the team is the opportunity to learn from your perspective. This builds Alex's awareness of the impact of not using the tools. However, awareness alone does not create change. Accountability turns awareness into action by helping the employee understand what's happening now and choose what to do next.  For example, you guide Alex to explore the current situation by asking what's making the tools hard to use right now. Then you help Alex uncover options with what feels like the realistic next step. From there, you encourage a self-chosen commitment by asking what's a small milestone you can commit to this week.  As a leader, coaching is both a mindset and a skill set. It's the shift from fixer to capability builder, from directing to guiding. Coaching works best when it's structured. So throughout this workbook, you'll use two models, star and grow. Star is your structure for creating awareness by providing clear specific and fact-based feedback.  Row is your structure for accountability by guiding the employee to generate options and commit to action. This is how awareness turns into accountability.
